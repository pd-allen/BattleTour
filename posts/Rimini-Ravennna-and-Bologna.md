## Arch of Augustus

The Arch of Augustus was built in 27 BC as the gateway to Via Flaminia,
the road linking Rimini to Rome, honouring Emperor Augustus. The Arch
was originally integrated into the walls surrounding Rimini. The walls
and surrounding buildings were demolished in 1936 to create a
free-standing monument, the oldest arch in Italy.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image4.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

The faces of Jupiter and Zeus adorn the outside of the arch.

Rimini was a major German supply and communication hub so it was bombed
373 times in 1943 and 1944, resulting in 98% of the buildings of the
city being damaged. Remarkably, the Arch was not damaged. A view of the
arch when the Allies captured the city. A disabled Calgary Regiment tank
in front of the Arch.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image5.png"
style="width:5.875in;height:8.33333in" />

During the German retreat, the order was given to destroy the Arch. The
following story explains how the Arch was saved.

> *Trageser refused to blow up the Arch of Augustus. The task had been
> entrusted to him by Lieutenant Renberg and was to be carried out by a
> young non-commissioned officer originally from the Sudetenland, whose
> name Trageser – in the conversation reported by Montemaggi – did not
> remember: "I personally gave the order not to blow up the Arch,
> assuming full responsibility for it... It seemed absurd to me to
> destroy a historical monument of this kind and achieve no result,
> given that the arch was isolated in the middle of a square and
> therefore traffic could have continued perfectly well, both to the
> right and left of the monument itself... "*
>
> *Interestingly, however, the Arch faced greater danger with the
> arrival of the British. Montemaggi, in fact, recalls the struggles
> that Public Works Councillor Mario Macina had to wage with British
> engineers to prevent the monumental stones from being used to
> consolidate the surface of the numerous roads destroyed by the tracks
> and wheels of thousands of vehicles.*
>
> *Once, the Arch was miraculously saved by Sergio Santarini , a middle
> school principal from Rimini, who happened to be passing by the
> monument and saw a team of black South Africans and mixed-race people
> from Mauritius, part of the Engineering Corps, who had already chained
> the monument to demolish it.*
>
> *Horrified, he ran to the offices of the Town Mayor (the British
> Governor) to report what he had seen. The British took action and
> cleared up the misunderstanding.*

## Tiberius Bridge

Another ancient landmark of Rimini is the Tiberius Bridge, one of the
best-preserved Roman bridges in the world completed in 21 AD, with 5
semicircular arches spanning 70 metres.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image6.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

Again, it was a miracle the bridge survived the war. The multiple
bombings did not severely damage the bridge. Also, during their retreat,
the Germans tried to blow up the Tiberius Bridge as they had all of the
other bridges in the area. There are several stories as to why the
bridge survived. One was the German Officer in charge refused to destroy
the ancient landmark, others included the bridge design was too robust,
the explosives were faulty or the Germans needed the bridge to escape
and waited too long to detonate.

A modern narrative source recounts that a German officer—identified as
Marshal Trek Esere—deliberately disobeyed the order to destroy the
bridge, sparing it as an act of personal defiance. This account
describes how the demolition cavities were prepared, but the officer
chose not to complete the destruction.

While this story is compelling, it appears in contemporary retellings
rather than primary wartime documentation. It should be treated as a
plausible but not definitively proven explanation.

## Malatestian Temple

The Malatestian Temple (*Tempio Malatestiano*) in Rimini is a landmark
of the Italian Renaissance, representing the ambitious transformation of
a 13th-century Gothic church into a monumental dynastic mausoleum.
Today, the building serves as the **Cathedral of Rimini** (dedicated to
St. Columba). It suffered significant damage during WWII bombings but
was meticulously restored in the 1950s.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image11.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

The temple was constructed at the direction of Sigismondo Malatesta in
honour of his lover and later wife, Isotta degli Atti. The construction
started in 1447 on the site of a 13<sup>th</sup> century church. The
chapel was constructed quickly but the proposed dome was never
constructed due to Sigismondo’s death in 1468, leading to an unfinished
look. The intertwined initials of I and S feature prominently as do
sculptures of Elephants, Sigismondo’s personal symbol. His tag line was
“Elephas Indus cullices non timet”. For those of you whose Latin is a
bit rusty: “The Indian Elephant does not fear mosquitoes”. This was a
shot at the Pope, who later had Sigismondo excommunicated.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image12.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

The I/S symbol is everywhere.

## Castel Sismondo

Castel Sismondo is a formidable 15th-century fortress located in the
heart of Rimini. Built by Sigismondo Pandolfo Malatesta (same guy as
previous building), the ruler of Rimini, it served as both a defensive
stronghold and a grand princely residence. Today, the castle has been
completely reimagined. It now serves as a primary wing of the Federico
Fellini Museum, dedicated to the legendary film director who was born in
Rimini.

### Domus del Chirurgo

The Domus del Chirurgo (Surgeon’s House) is an extraordinary
archaeological site in Rimini, often described as a "little Pompeii"
because of its remarkable state of preservation. A purpose-built
structure now protects the discovery.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image18.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

Discovered in 1989 during renovations to the Piazza Ferrari, this Roman
villa provides a rare, frozen-in-time look at medical life in the 3rd
century AD. A fire in the late 3<sup>rd</sup> century destroyed the
building, collapsed the roof and buried rooms under the rubble. The site
subsequently became a garden, an orchard then an open space, so no heavy
construction destroyed the Roman ruins.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image19.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:8.63264in" />

Along with well preserved mosaics, a 150-piece surgical kit was also
discovered. Remarkably precise tools were found during the archeological
dig.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image20.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:2.70903in" />

## **Murales del borgo San Giuliano**

The Murales del Borgo San Giuliano are a vibrant collection of wall

Once a poor, marginalized neighborhood, Borgo San Giuliano has
transformed into one of the most picturesque and "Instagrammable" areas

## Roman Amphitheatre

The Roman Amphitheatre in Rimini was a massive 2<sup>nd</sup> century
arena built under Emperor Hadrian, confirmed with a coin bearing his
likeness embedded in the masonry. The Amphitheatre of holding
10,000–12,000 spectators, and today only fragments survive due to
medieval reuse and WWII bombing.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image23.png"
style="width:6.5in;height:3.17917in" />

Constructed in brick with a classic elliptical plan, similar in
proportions to the Colosseum.

- Major axis: 118 m

- Minor axis: 88 m

- Arena: ~74 × 45 m

- Height: 16–17 m

- Exterior portico: 60 arches

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image24.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

This scale made it one of the largest amphitheatres in northern Italy
and the only one still partially surviving in Emilia‑Romagna. The
amphitheatre was built into the defensive walls of Rimini and after the
decline of the Roman Empire used as a quarry for other buildings. It was
rediscovered in the 1840’s but severe damage caused by WW2 bombing mean
only fragments remain today.

## Ravenna

Ravenna was started as an Adriatic port city, connected to the sea by
canals. In 402 AD the capital of the Western Roman Empire was moved from
Milan to Ravenna because of the marshy ground surrounding it was a
natural defence and the canals provided easy naval access. The capital
was moved from Rome to Milan in 286 AD as it was closer to the northern
frontiers where most of the Military crises were occurring and was
easier to defend than Rome.

Wealth and artisans flowed into Ravenna, laying the foundations for the
early Christian mosaic traditions. The Mausoleum of Galla Placidia was
built around 425 AD

## Basilica di San Petronio

The Basilica di San Petronio is Bologna’s great unfinished Gothic
Basilica, begun in 1390 as a massive civic church intended to surpass
St. Peter’s in Rome, but never completed due to political, financial,
and architectural obstacles.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image43.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.89444in" />

The Basilica design is Italian Gothic with a Capacity of approximately
28,000 people (one of the largest churches in Europe)

The unfinished façade is one of Bologna’s most recognizable features.
Only the lower marble portion was completed; the upper half remains
brick.

## Archiginnasio Library

The most famous library in Bologna and one of the most important in all
of Italy is the Archiginnasio Library (Biblioteca Comunale
dell’Archiginnasio). It is the historic library of the University of
Bologna, the oldest university in Europe (founded 1088). It holds over
850,000 volumes, including rare manuscripts, incunabula (books printed
before 1501), and early printed books.

The library sits inside the Archiginnasio Palace, one of Bologna’s most
beautiful Renaissance buildings.

# Santo Stefano Complex

Bologna’s most distinctive collection of churches is the Santo Stefano
complex, also known as the Sette Chiese (“Seven Churches”), a unique
cluster of interconnected religious buildings forming a single sacred
labyrinth. It is the city’s most unusual religious site: a fusion of
multiple churches, courtyards, cloisters, and monastic structures built
across more than 1,500 years.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image54.png"
style="width:6.5in;height:3.65625in" />

Although popularly called the Seven Churches, the number has changed
over time. Historically seven, today four main churches remain, but the
name persists because of the symbolic and architectural unity of the
site.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image55.png"
style="width:6.5in;height:3.65833in" />

The walking map shows how the churches are linked together.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image56.png"
style="width:6.5in;height:7.86597in" />

The complex began in the **5th century**, when **Bishop Petronius**
built a church over the remains of a Roman temple dedicated to the
goddess Isis. His intention, according to tradition, was to recreate the
sacred sites of Jerusalem—especially the Holy Sepulchre—within Bologna.

Over the centuries, **Franks**, **Lombards**, and later **Benedictine
monks** expanded and reshaped the complex (10<sup>th</sup>
–13<sup>th</sup> centuries), adding chapels, cloisters, and monastic
buildings.

## The main components of the Santo Stefano complex

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image57.png"
style="width:6.5in;height:4.52917in" />

Below is an overview of the principal structures within the collection:

- **Chiesa del Crocifisso (Church of the Crucifix)**  
  A 7<sup>th</sup> century Lombard church, austere in style, containing
  a 14<sup>th</sup> century crucifix by Simone dei Crocifissi.

- **Chiesa del Santo Sepolcro (Church of the Holy Sepulchre)**  
  Octagonal, built to imitate the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem;
  originally from the 5<sup>th</sup> century, rebuilt in the
  12<sup>th</sup>.

- **Chiesa dei Santi Vitale e Agricola**  
  Houses the sarcophagi of Bologna’s early Christian martyrs Vitale and
  Agricola.

- **Chiesa della Trinità (Church of the Trinity)**  
  Reworked in the 12<sup>th</sup> –13<sup>th</sup> centuries; notable
  for its medieval wooden sculptures and Romanesque capitals.

- **Cortile di Pilato (Courtyard of Pilate)**  
  Contains an 8<sup>th</sup> ‑century marble basin donated by Lombard
  kings Liutprand and Hildeprand.

- **Medieval Cloister**  
  A two‑level Romanesque cloister (10<sup>th</sup>–13<sup>th</sup>
  centuries), one of the finest in Emilia‑Romagna.

- **Santo Stefano Museum**  
  Displays artworks from the complex, including works by Vitale da
  Bologna and Simone dei Crocifissi.

# Final Thoughts

The most exotic meal I had was a local delicacy, Mediterranean style
Turbot. The entire fish was delivered on a giant platter, covered with
sauce and potatoes. The fish was delicious, but Turbot, being a flat
fish, is very thin so it was an adventure digging out the meat from the
many, many bones. I enjoyed a lot of seafood in many forms, but this was
the most unique.

<img src="assets/Rimini-Ravennna-and-Bologna/media/image62.jpeg"
style="width:6.5in;height:8.66667in" />

It was a great trip to Italy. The food was wonderful everywhere we ate
and any of the tours we did, Battlefield Touring, visit to San Marino or
turning the ancient Roman cities of Rimini, Ravenna and Bologna would
have made for a great trip. The weather was spectacular and the visits
were magnificent.

Touring the ancient cities of Rimini, Ravenna and Bologna and seeing
structures that are 2,000 years old gives you an appreciation of what
old really means. The craftmanship and engineering knowledge to create
structures that last millennia and survive multiple regime changes and
innumerable wars is a testament to skills of the ancients.

This is not a location that is top of anyone’s list of a must visit in
Italy, but combination of ancient and military history and the
collection of another tiny country made this one of our top travels.
